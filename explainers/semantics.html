Semantics in AI

How AI systems represent and reason about meaning — the foundation of everything from search to ChatGPT.

Semantics is the study of meaning. In AI, it refers to how systems represent, reason about, and work with the meaning of language — not just its surface form. It's the difference between pattern-matching text and understanding it.

Syntactic (surface)

Treats text as a sequence of characters. Knows 'cat' and 'cat' are the same. Doesn't know 'cat' and 'feline' are related.

Semantic (meaning)

Knows that 'cat', 'feline', and 'kitty' are semantically close. Knows 'deposit' means different things in banking vs. geology.

The jump from keyword search to ChatGPT is fundamentally a jump from syntactic to semantic processing. Everything powerful about modern AI rests on this foundation.

Meaning vs Syntax

Short: Semantics is about what words mean, not how they're spelled or arranged.

Detail: A syntax-only system sees 'bank' as 6 characters. A semantic system understands that 'bank' means something different beside a river than in a financial sentence.

Examples:

  • "I went to the bank to deposit money" → financial institution
  • "The river bank was muddy" → land beside water
  • Same word, completely different meaning

Embeddings

Short: The way AI converts words and sentences into lists of numbers that encode meaning.

Detail: An embedding maps every word/sentence to a point in a high-dimensional space. Words with similar meanings land near each other. This is how AI 'understands' similarity without rules.

Examples:

  • "dog" and "puppy" land close together in embedding space
  • "king" - "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen" (vector arithmetic)
  • Search engines use this to find relevant docs, not just keyword matches

Context Sensitivity

Short: The same word means different things depending on the words around it.

Detail: Modern AI handles this with attention mechanisms — each word 'attends' to every other word in the sentence, building meaning that's sensitive to full context.

Examples:

  • "Make it pop" means different things to a designer vs. a chemist
  • "I can't recommend him highly enough" = strong praise or criticism?
  • Context resolves ambiguity that syntax alone cannot

Intent Recognition

Short: Understanding what a user actually wants, not just the literal words they typed.

Detail: Semantic understanding means 'fix my code' and 'my code is broken, help' are the same request. AI maps surface-level phrasing to underlying user intent.

Examples:

  • "How do I get rid of this headache?" → pain relief advice
  • Search: "cheap flights London" → user intent is to book, not just browse
  • "Cancel my order" and "I want a refund" → same intent

Semantic Search

Desc: Find documents by meaning, not just keyword overlap.

How: Query and documents are both embedded. The closest vectors in meaning are returned — even with zero word overlap.

Example: Search 'heart attack' returns results about 'myocardial infarction'

Chatbots

Desc: Understand what users mean across all the ways they might phrase something.

How: Intent classification maps user utterances to defined intents via semantic similarity.

Example: "Cancel my order", "I want a refund", "Stop my subscription" → same intent

Summarisation

Desc: Condense long text by identifying the semantically core ideas.

How: The model identifies which sentences carry the most meaning and removes paraphrase and repetition.

Example: 10-page report → 3-paragraph summary preserving all key decisions

Translation

Desc: Preserve meaning across languages, not just word-for-word substitution.

How: Neural models learn cross-lingual semantic spaces where equivalent meanings cluster together.

Example: Idioms, tone, and cultural nuance are preserved, not just words

Anomaly Detection

Desc: Find text that doesn't match expected semantic patterns.

How: Documents are embedded and outliers in semantic space are flagged for review.

Example: Customer email saying "excellent, never using you again" flagged as anomalous

▼
Meaning is not grounded
AI semantics is statistical correlation, not true understanding. It finds patterns between words without understanding what those words refer to in the physical world. Analogy: Knows 'fire is hot' correlates with 'fire causes burns' but has never felt heat.
▼
Ambiguity can fool it
When context is thin or novel, semantic models pick the statistically likely interpretation — which may be wrong in edge cases. Analogy: Like assuming every 'bank' question is about money, because that's more common in training data.
▼
Bias in embeddings
If training data has human biases — gender, race, cultural assumptions — embeddings encode and amplify those biases. Analogy: "Doctor" embedding landed closer to "man" than "woman" in older models, reflecting historical data skew.
